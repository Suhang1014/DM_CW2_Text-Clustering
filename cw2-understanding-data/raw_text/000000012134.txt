Similar views were expressedby Publius Crassus Mucianus (P. 56), the Pontifex Maxi-mus of the day, who was held in universal honour by thesenate and the citizens as a man and a jurist. Even hisbrother Publius Mucius Scaevola, the founder of scientificjurisprudence in Rome, seemed not averse to the plan ofreform; and his voice was of the greater weight, as he stoodin some measure aloof from either party. Similar were thesentiments of Quintus Metellus, the conqueror of Macedoniaand of the Achaeans, but respected not so much on accountof his warlike deeds as because he was a model of the olddiscipline and manners alike in his domestic and his publiclife. Tiberius Gracchus was closely connected with thesemen, particularly with Appius whose daughter he hadmarried, and with Mucianus whose daughter was marriedto his brother.
