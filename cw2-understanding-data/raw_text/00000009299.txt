As he hadfor some time foreseen this event, he had ships alreadyprepared: dismissing therefore a useless crowd ofsoldiers, under the appearance of garrisons, into thetowns of Bruttium, a few of which adhered to him ra­ther through fear than affection, he carried over toAfrica such of the troops as were fit for service. Agreat number of natives of Italy, refusing to followhim to Africa, and flying to the sanctuary of JunoLacinia, which, till that day, had never been violated,were barbarously put to death within the walls of thetemple. We are told, that hardly any person evershowed more grief on leaving his native soil to go intoexile, than Hannibal on his departure from the coun­try of his enemy; that he often looked back on thecoasts of Italy, inveighing against gods and men, ut­tering curses on his own head for not having led hismen to Rome, yet reeking with blood from the slaugh­ter at Cannx: reflecting, with the bitterest vexation,that Scipio, who since his appointment to the consul­ship, had not looked in the face of the Carthaginianenemy in Italy, had yet spirit to go and attack Car­thage; while he, who had slain a hundred thousandfighting men at Thrasymenus and Cannae, had sufferedbis strength to moulder away about Casilinum, Cuma?,and Nola.
