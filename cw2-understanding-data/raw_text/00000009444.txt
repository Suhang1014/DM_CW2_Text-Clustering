On the Alban mount a statueof Jupiter, and a tree, standing near the temple; atOstia, a grove; at Capua, a wall, and the temple ofFortune; and at Sinuessa, a wall and gate, were struckby lightning. Farther, it was reported that the Albanwater flowed in a bloody stream; that, at Rome, in thecell of the temple of Fors Fortuna, an image, whichwas in the crown of the goddess, fell from her headinto her hands; that an ox spoke at Privernum; thata vulture, while the forum was crowded, flew downinto one of the shops; and that, at Sinuessa, an infantwas born whose sex was doubtful, such as are com­monly called in Greek (a language more manageablethan ours, particularly in the compounding of words,)Androgynes; that a shower of milk fell; and that aboy was born with the head of an elephant. Theseprodigies were expiated with the larger kind of vic­tims. Orders were given for a supplication to be per­formed at all the shrines, and prayers to be offeredduring one day, for the averting of misfortunes; anda decree passed that the pretor, Caius Hostilius, shouldvow and celebrate the games of Apollo, in like manneras they had of late years been vowed and celebrated.At the same time, the consul Quintus Fulvius held anassembly for the election of censors. The censorschosen were men who had never yet been consuls,Marcus Cornelius Cethegus and Publius SemproniusTuditanus. By direction of the senate the questionwas proposed to the people; and the people orderedthat these, by their censorial authority, should let tofarm the lands of Campania. The choosing of the se­nate was delayed by a dispute between the censorsabout the nomination of the prince of it: the makingthe choice had fallen, by lot, to Sempronius; but Cor­nelius alleged that he ought to observe the practicehanded down from their ancestors, which was to ap­point as prince the person who in the list of censorsstood the first of any then living, and this was TitusManlius Torquatus.
