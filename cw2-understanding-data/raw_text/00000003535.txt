The fate of thesevaliant men deserves to be more distinctly noticed.Seven hundred had perished in the siege, two thou­sand three hundred survived to defend the breach.One thousand and seventy were destroyed with fireand sword in the last assault; and if seven hundredand thirty were made prisoners, only eighteen amongthem were found without the marks of honourablewounds. The remaining five hundred escaped intothe citadel, which they maintained without any hopesof relief, rejecting the fairest terms of capitulationand service, till they were lost in the flames. Theydied in obedience to the commands of their prince;and such examples of loyalty and valour might excitetheir countrymen to deeds of equal despair and moreprosperous event.
