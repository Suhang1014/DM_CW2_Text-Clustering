By his po­pular acts he gained the affections of thepeople, with the title of Augustus, theFather of his Country. Scythia, Sar-matia, the Garamantes and Bactrians, In­dia, and the people called the Seres, sub­mitted to his authority, and sent their am­bassadors to settle the terms of a generalpeace. At Rome, and the capital cities ofthe provinces, temples, orders of priest­hood, sacerdotal colleges, were dedicatedto him, not only after his death, but, inmany places, during his life. He died atNola on the 18th of August, A.U. 767.His character, strictly examined, was moresplendid for his policy than his virtues.He owed his elevation to the vices of Le-pidus and Anthony, and the abilities ofVipsanius Agrippa; but it redounds to hispraise, that, what he gained by the prud­ence and valour of others he was able tosupport, by a well-judged system of po­licy, during a space of four-and-forty years
