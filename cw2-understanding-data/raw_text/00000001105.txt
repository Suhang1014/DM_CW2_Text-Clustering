Themonarch who resigned to his subjects the superiorityof merit and virtue, was distinguished by the op­pressive magnificence of his dress, his table, hisbuildings, and his train. The stately palaces erectedby Constantine and his sons were decorated withmany coloured marbles, and ornaments of massygold. The most exquisite dainties were procured,to gratify their pride, rather than their taste; birdsof the most distant climates, fish from the most re­mote seas, fruits out of their natural season, winterroses, and summer snows. The domestic crowd ofthe palace surpassed the expense of the legions; yetthe smallest part of this costly multitude was subser­vient to the use, or even to the splendour, of thethrone. The monarch was disgraced, and the peoplewas injured, by the creation and sale of an infinitenumber of obscure, and even titular employments;and the most worthless of mankind might purchasethe privilege of being maintained, without the ne­cessity of labour, from the public revenue.
