They were fullof hopes to bring into their subjection an island by nomeans inconsiderable either for number of mariners orsoldiers; and at the fame time judged themselves able,by their present alliances, to defray the expence of theirfleet without requesting pay from Tiflaphemes. Ac­cordingly, this winter, with great dispatch, they put tosea from Cnidus; and, arriving first at Camirus, onthe Rhodian coast, with ninety-four ships, they strucka consternation into the multitude, who knew nothingof past transactions, and were the sooner tempted to a-bandon their dwellings as the city was not guarded bythe least fortification. The Lacedæmonians, after­wards, summoning to a conference these, and the Rho-dians also from two other cities, Lindus and lelysus,persuaded them to revolt from the Athenians.
